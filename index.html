1. Setup
How to Use This Simulation

An Accountable Care Organization (ACO) is an entity that participates in Value-Based Care contracts with CMS (Medicare) and/or Medicare Advantage/Commercial health plans.

This simulation models Medicare Advantage and Commercial ACO dynamics where payers operate under market pressures. Medicare MSSP programs may differ—CMS actively adjusts rules to support ACO success.

  1. Start with "Realistic" — Use the Realistic preset for a grounded baseline
  2. Progress through each step — Each reveals a new layer of the financial reality
  3. Change one input at a time — See how each parameter affects the outcome
  4. Watch the money flow — Follow the savings from payer to physician and see where it goes

Why a PHO?

💵 Payer Advance Parameters
How Payer Advance Works

The payer provides a monthly Per-Member-Per-Month (PMPM) payment during the 18-month operations period. If the ACO hits its targets, this advance is deducted from the ACO share before distribution. If targets are missed, the payer can claw back a percentage of the advance from future FFS revenue.

Note: Clawback is split evenly among participating PCPs.

PMPM Advance ($) i Monthly per-member-per-month payment from payer during operations. Higher PMPM means more upfront cash but larger deduction from ACO share.
$10
Clawback % (if missed) i Percentage of advance the payer reclaims if ACO misses targets. 100% means full clawback; lower percentages mean the payer absorbs some loss.
75%
Annual PMPM Ratchet % i Percentage by which PMPM is reduced each year during contract renewals. 100% ratchet means PMPM drops to $0 (discontinued) after Year 1.
75%

6 📈 Multi-Year Projection
Funding Path: Bank Loan

Project ACO outcomes across multiple years to see long-term financial trajectory.

📖 Understanding This Projection ▼
How Ratcheting Works ▼

Benchmark Ratcheting (TCOC): Each year the ACO hits its savings target, payers reduce next year's benchmark by the ratchet percentage. This shrinks achievable savings over time until the ACO cannot meet the MSR threshold.

Quality Gate Ratcheting: Payers also increase quality requirements annually. Meanwhile, ACOs have a ceiling on achievable quality. When the gate exceeds this ceiling, quality failure becomes inevitable.

Key Insight: Quality is an independent failure point. An ACO can optimize costs perfectly and still fail if quality requirements outpace improvement capability. This dual-squeeze represents the full picture of ACO sustainability risk.

Real-World Context: Medicare vs MA/Commercial ▼

Medicare MSSP: CMS has a vested interest in ACO success and regularly adjusts program parameters (benchmarks, quality thresholds, shared savings rates) to prevent widespread failure. Historical data shows CMS intervening when too many ACOs struggle.

Medicare Advantage & Commercial Payers: Private payers face quarterly earnings pressure. During contract renewals, they have an interest in ratcheting benchmarks and raising quality thresholds—capturing prior savings as the new baseline and making it progressively harder for ACOs to earn shared savings. This simulation models those dynamics.

7 🎲 Monte Carlo Analysis

Monte Carlo simulation stress-tests the ACO model by running thousands of iterations with varied parameters.

How It Works ▼

Year 1 Simulation

Runs 1,000 iterations of Year 1, each time sampling different values for market conditions and costs.

  • Fixed by default: Contract terms (savings target, MSR, quality gate) and network size—known quantities when an ACO signs a contract
  • Varied by default: TCOC per patient, infrastructure costs, practice burden, funder-specific parameters, and RAF growth rates
  • Customizing: Uncheck any "fixed" variable to randomize it; check any "varied" variable to hold it constant

Multi-Year Simulation

Each iteration randomizes starting conditions once, then runs a deterministic multi-year projection. Year-to-year dynamics carry forward from each year's outcome:

  • Benchmark ratcheting — Payers reduce next year's benchmark after successful years
  • Reserve dynamics — Profits accumulate; losses deplete reserves. Exhaustion triggers failure
  • RAF compounding — ACO and regional RAF grow deterministically from sampled initial rates
  • Quality gate ratcheting — Payers increase thresholds; ACO improvement may not keep pace

Sampling Methods

  • Uniform (default) — Values spread evenly across the entire range
  • Triangular — Values cluster around the base scenario with lower probability at extremes
  • Multi-Year uses Quasi-Monte Carlo — Sobol sequences provide better coverage; 500 iterations ≈ 2,000 random samples in accuracy
